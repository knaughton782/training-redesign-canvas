The Value of Engagement

Student engagement and interaction - with the students and between the students - increases satisfaction, enhances motivation to learn, and improves performance in virtual courses.

Student engagement is defined as “the student’s psychological investment in and effort directed toward learning, understanding, or mastering the knowledge, skills, or crafts that academic work is intended to promote” (Newmann, Wehlage, & Lamborn, 1992, p. 12).

The content for your course will be intentionally designed for maximum student engagement. Engagement is crucial to student learning and satisfaction in the online environment.

The content in your course can consist of lecture-type notes, videos, and diagrams to enhance learning. These materials (intentionally designed by a selected faculty member) are presented to your students BEFORE your class meeting so you can start the meeting engaging with the students by asking questions, clarifying concepts, and working on activities.

 

Top 3 tips for interacting with students in a virtual environment from the 2022 Teaching Festival:

  1. Focus on student success by setting your expectations (weekly due dates, rubrics, participation, etc.) and then reminding students frequently about how to be successful.
  2. Establish patterns of communication to support students. When will you be available? When and how will you provide feedback? Video and audio feedback makes it feel more personalized.
  3. Encourage students to take ownership of their learning. Let the students … encourage the students … to talk and interact as a way of developing their learning community. Your job is to facilitate the learning that happens.

 

Types of Interactions

  • Student to Student
  • Student to Instructor
  • Student to Content

Student to Student

 

Interactions between students can include social as well as course related collaborations. Social interaction in the very beginning of the course can increase student's comfort and trust with each other and with the environment. These types of interactions set the stage for the development of your learning community.

Student-to-student interaction-based activities can include:

  • Discussions and debates
  • Working on a collaborative document
  • Working as a group on projects or case studies
  • Peer reviews of selected work

These types of examples can be used for project groups doing research and presentations or maybe a discussion question where students brainstorm alternatives to a problem posed by you or the textbook.

Student to Instructor

Student-to-instructor interaction can include both direct instruction (think: instructional notes and demos) and other more informal mentoring and support. Regular and substantive student-to-instructor interaction is required for courses to be classified as "online courses" and not "correspondence courses" by the US Department of Education and the Higher Learning Commission.

Some ideas for student-to-instructor interaction that can be built into the course include:

  • Incorporate the opportunity for instructors to provide feedback on assignments and activities
  • Include expectations for students that instructors will be participating in discussions
  • Create some example announcements for instructors to use so they can posting regular and frequent announcements to summarize previous week or prep for the next week
  • Providing instructions for incorporating virtual office hours

Student to Content

Student-to-content interaction includes students' actual interactions with the instructional materials as well as their more abstract interactions with the concepts and ideas presented. It is more than just reading the chapters or watching a YouTube video. Interaction with the content can happen when:

  • Tutorials (using text, still images, audio, and/ or video) are present for the students to consume
  • Formative quizzes are incorporated and include useful and usable feedback
  • Clear and detailed directions are given to investigate a topic on the Internet
  • Reflections on required reading or watching a required resource
  • Simulations and scenarios are included to help student understanding of a topic

Opportunities for each of these types of interactions and engagements are included within the course you will be teaching. All of these types of interactions are important pieces of the learning environment that you are creating and maintaining throughout the term.
